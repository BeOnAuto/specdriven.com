# The Question Isn't Whether AI Can Code. It's Whether You Can Specify.

![The Question Isn't Whether AI Can Code. It's Whether You Can Specify.](/images/perspectives/the-question-isnt-whether-ai-can-code-its-whether-you-can-specify.png)

Give it a few years and building software without specifications will look as reckless as deploying without version control.

Not because specs are trendy, but because the way AI-assisted development works demands them.

AI doesn't hold your design in its head. Every session is a clean slate. Every prompt is a newborn with no memory of your constraints, your decisions, your trade-offs. That's not a temporary limitation. That's how the tools work.

Without specs, you're handing an infinitely productive amnesiac the keys to a complex system. It'll produce. Endlessly. Without coherence.

With specs, you change the equation. You give it memory. A record of every decision that matters. Constraints. Intent. And you give yourself verification: the confidence that the system still works as intended while it undergoes constant change.

The practices that will define what comes next aren't new. They're 60 years of hard-won insight, refined.

TDD taught us to record design decisions as executable expectations. BDD taught us shared understanding in language the whole team can read. DDD taught us that words shape systems. Event Sourcing taught us to record facts, not just state. Event Storming taught us to model through time.

All of these are converging. Into spec dialects. Into executable specification frameworks. Into tools that compress design-to-implementation to minutes.

I've spent nearly two decades in this space. Coined "spec-driven development" in 2016. Built Narrative-Driven Development as a synthesis of everything I've learned. Created specdriven.com as a neutral home for the community, because this is bigger than any one tool. And I'm building Auto to make these ideas accessible to every product engineer.

But this series was never about me or my tools. It was about a principle.

Capture your intent. Don't let decisions live only in your head or a chat window.

Specify your design. Be precise. Separate requirements from specs from tasks.

Verify continuously. Make specs executable. Let automation confirm the system still meets them.

Build with confidence. Know, don't hope, that the system does what it should.

The spec-driven future isn't a question of if. It's a question of whether you lead it or spend the next few years catching up.

Thanks for following The Spec-Driven Shift. If it changed how you think about building software, share it with your team. And if you want to go deeper, reach out!
